The Principle

Weatherwave Space, Inc. was founded on a simple idea: honest engineering, clear thinking, and disciplined iteration can outperform bureaucracy. We are building a meritocracy in practice, not in name. At Weatherwave, ideas win because they are right. Every design is justified, every decision is measured, and every improvement is earned. We believe agility and rigor are not opposites; they are the foundation of sustainable excellence. Our systems, from spacecraft architecture to team structure, are built to learn continuously and improve relentlessly.

At Weatherwave Space, we measure success by what we build and how honestly we build it. We are proving that small, intelligent teams can achieve flagship-class performance for a fraction of the cost. Small satellites, working together, can change the world.

Weatherwave Leadership

Jason Derleth, Founder and CEO

Before Jason ever worked on spacecraft, he taught himself how to build violins and cellos by reading books on the subject and working until they sang. That process taught Jason precision, structure, patience, and feedback: the same principles that drive good engineering. His undergraduate studies at St. John’s College, Annapolis, in philosophy and the history of math and science taught him how ideas evolve, how discovery happens, and how to reason from first principles. He also studied comparative literature and the classics, grounding technical work in a broader understanding of human creativity and persistence.

Jason went to MIT not for a credential, but to learn how to build spacecraft. He earned a master’s in Aero/Astro Engineering, despite having no formal engineering training beforehand. He supplemented that technical education with courses in business dynamics and business strategy, gaining a broader perspective on how to lead and sustain complex organizations. Jason learned that engineering is not just about machines, but rather about systems of people, time, and truth that build them.

At NASA JPL, he served as Deputy Systems Engineer for the Curiosity Mars Rover in its formulation phase. The NASA Administrator asked Jason to come to NASA HQ to be a core member of the Exploration Systems Architecture Study, where his team worked to develop the Agency’s plans to return to the Moon. For over a decade, he worked as Program Manager and then Program Executive of the NASA Innovative Advanced Concepts (NIAC) program, where he helped develop a community of advanced thinkers that is still innovating today. Jason later co-led the initial technology portfolio for the Habitable Worlds Observatory, the $10 billion follow-on to JWST, as Chief Technologist for the Physics of the Cosmos and Cosmic Origins programs.

After decades of helping large programs and small projects succeed, Jason founded Weatherwave Space to return to building, and to once again build a community of advanced thinkers that will change how spacecraft are built.
